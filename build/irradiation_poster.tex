\block{The ATLAS Tile Calorimeter \& the HL-LHC Upgrade}{
    \looseitems
        \item As the primary hadronic sampling calorimeter of ATLAS, TileCal
        (built from steel absorbers and scintillating plastic tiles, covering $|\eta|<1.7$)
        is crucial for measuring hadronic jets, missing transverse energy,
        hadronically decaying $\tau$-leptons, and for muon identification.
        \item To cope with the unprecedented instantaneous luminosity and pile-up
        of the \textbf{High-Luminosity LHC}, the entire on-detector electronics system
        is being \textbf{fully replaced}. This upgrade enables fully continuous,
        un-triggered digital read-out of all channels at \textbf{40\,MHz} with
        enhanced energy and timing resolution.
        \item This new hardware faces a vastly harsher radiation environment.
        Consequently, every single component must be rigorously qualified against
        \textbf{\textcolor{tid}{Total Ionizing Dose (TID)}},
        \textbf{\textcolor{niel}{Non-Ionizing Energy Loss (NIEL)}}, and
        \textbf{\textcolor{see}{Single Event Effects (SEE/SEL)}}.
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{0.12cm}
    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{assets/fig_minidrawer_blockdiagram.png}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{-0.2cm}
    {\small TileCal Phase-II Minidrawer (left) and the on-/off-detector read-out
    chain (right).}
}

\block{Read-Out Chain \& Radiation Requirements}{
    \looseitems
        \item The on-detector electronics are partitioned into \textbf{896 Minidrawers (MDs)}.
        Each MD processes signals from up to 12 PMTs at 40\,MHz. The MD houses
        Front-End Boards (signal shaping), a \textbf{Mainboard (MB)} (digitization),
        and a \textbf{Daughterboard (DB)} (control, clock distribution, and high-speed data transmission).
        Power is supplied by the \textbf{Low-Voltage Power Supply (LVPS)}.
        \item Digital streams are transmitted off-detector via multi-Gbps optical links
        to FELIX and the Tile PreProcessor (TilePPr).
        \item Radiation criteria are based on ATLAS simulations for the
        \emph{most-exposed} regions, scaled by rigorous safety factors (SF):
        \textbf{1.5} (simulation uncertainty), \textbf{$\times$3--5} (low-dose-rate effects / lot-to-lot variation),
        and \textbf{1.3} (non-monoenergetic beams).
        \item Final Daughterboard targets (with SFs applied):
        \textbf{\textcolor{tid}{TID $\approx$ 108\,Gy}},
        \textbf{\textcolor{niel}{NIEL $\approx 13.16\times10^{12}$ n/cm$^2$}},
        \textbf{\textcolor{see}{SEE $\approx 4.54\times10^{11}$ h/cm$^2$}}.
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{0.12cm}
    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.99\linewidth]{assets/fig_radiation_criteria.pdf}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{-0.2cm}
    {\small Worked example for the LVPS bricks: simulated dose/fluence scaled by
    safety factors to the qualification target.}
}

\block{The Link \& Control Daughterboard (DB6)}{
    \tightitems
        \item Serving as the communications hub, the DB delivers the LHC-synchronized clock
        and configuration to the front-end, while streaming digitized MB data
        off-detector through high-speed optical links.
        \item The sixth revision (\textbf{DB6}) achieves exceptional radiation
        tolerance via a \textbf{double-redundant} architecture (A/B halves).
        Robustness is ensured by \textbf{Triple Modular Redundancy (TMR)},
        \textbf{Xilinx Soft Error Mitigation (SEM)}, \textbf{GBT-FEC}
        error correction, and SEL-protection circuitry.
        \item Core components include 4$\times$ commercial \textbf{SFP+} transceivers
        (two 4.8\,Gbps downlinks, four 9.6\,Gbps uplinks), custom CERN \textbf{GBTx} ASICs,
        Xilinx \textbf{Kintex UltraScale (KU)} and Microsemi \textbf{ProASIC3} FPGAs.
        COTS parts are utilized wherever feasible.
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{0cm}
    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.55\linewidth]{assets/fig_db6_blockdiagram.png}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{-0.3cm}
    {\small DB6 block diagram: two redundant halves, KU + ProASIC FPGAs, GBTx and
    four SFP+ optical links.}
}

\block{DB6 Radiation Qualification: TID, NIEL \& SEL}{
    \tightitems
        \item Comprehensive testing was performed on four prototype boards (DB6-0 to DB6-3).
        \item \textbf{\textcolor{tid}{TID}}: DB6-1 and DB6-2 underwent $\gamma$-irradiation at the CERN
        CC60 ($^{60}$Co) facility to 220\,Gy (3.37\,Gy/h) and 54\,Gy (0.33\,Gy/h).
        DB6-3 was exposed to 54\,MeV protons at IFJ Kraków to $\sim$400\,Gy (2.88\,Gy/h).
        Temperatures and currents remained stable, confirming no TID-induced damage.
        \item \textbf{\textcolor{niel}{NIEL}}: DB6-0 was irradiated at the TRIGA reactor (JSI Ljubljana)
        to $14\times10^{12}$ n$_\text{eq}$/cm$^2$. The KU FPGAs exhibited strong tolerance, while
        the ProASIC and FLASH firmware recovered full functionality after annealing.
        \item \textbf{\textcolor{sel}{SEL}}: Testing with 54\,MeV protons ($2.62\times10^{11}$ p/cm$^2$)
        and 226\,MeV protons ($1.11\times10^{12}$ p/cm$^2$) yielded \textbf{zero single-event latch-ups}.
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{0cm}
    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.54\linewidth]{assets/fig_db6_tid_test.png}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{-0.3cm}
    {\small DB6-3 board currents and FPGA temperature, stable over the full
    $\sim$400\,Gy proton TID run (both halves).}
}

\block{Component Results \& Production}{
    \tightitems
        \item \textbf{SFP+ Transceivers}: AVAGO devices failed completely, and 1/9 CORETEK
        modules failed at $9\times10^{12}$ n/cm$^2$. \textbf{Direktronik 33-4248} and
        \textbf{FS-Europe SFP-10GSR-85} maintained full functionality and were chosen.
        \item \textbf{Design Iterations}: \textbf{FDFMA3N109 MOSFETs} showed threshold shifts,
        prompting removal of the SEL over-current circuit. Also, 4/15 \textbf{LTC6902}
        oscillators failed and were eliminated. INA333, Schottky diodes, and
        540BAA oscillators passed successfully.
        \item \textbf{Final Status}: All essential DB6 components are fully qualified.
        Eliminating radiation-sensitive components yielded a simpler, robust design.
        $\sim$\textbf{930 Daughterboards} will be produced (Stockholm Univ.) in \textbf{2026--2027}.
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{0cm}

    \end{center}
    \vspace{-0.3cm}

    cavity flux map (right).}
}

\block{LVPS Bricks \& Design Optimization}{
    \tightitems
        \item Located in the module \emph{fingers}, the LVPS experiences the most severe
        radiation environment within TileCal. The Phase-II design deploys \textbf{8 independent DC-DC ``bricks''}
        per module. Each brick is a 300\,kHz two-switch forward converter that steps
        \textbf{200\,V down to 10\,V} ($\sim$23\,W). This 8-way segmentation boosts system reliability
        and isolates potential localized failures.
        \item \textbf{Enhanced Efficiency}: Replacing the STB57N65M5 power MOSFET with the
        \textbf{SIHFS9N60A} drastically improved the brick's efficiency from $\sim$58\% to
        \textbf{$\sim$72\%}. This comfortably surpasses the $\geq$65\% threshold demanded by the
        77\,kW cooling infrastructure, aided by a 65\% reduction in gate--source capacitance.
        Furthermore, a lower-resistance output inductor was integrated to minimize thermal dissipation.
        \item \textbf{Simplified Control Wiring}: The control lines were halved from \textbf{18} (legacy system)
        down to just \textbf{9} signals. This was achieved by introducing a tri-state logic scheme
        (turn-on / turn-off / keep-state) sharing a common return, significantly simplifying routing from the service cavern.
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{0.1cm}

        \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{assets/fig_lvps_brick.pdf}\\[2mm]

\block{LVPS Radiation Qualification}{
    \tightitems
        \item Components were scrutinized for \textbf{\textcolor{tid}{TID}} (CC60/PSI),
        \textbf{\textcolor{niel}{NIEL}} (TRIGA), and \textbf{\textcolor{see}{SEE}}
        (PIF). Assembled bricks were validated in \textbf{system tests at CERN CHARM}.
        \item Critical parts (e.g., 7 diodes, \textbf{LT1681}, \textbf{LTC6241},
        \textbf{LT3080}, \textbf{IR2110}, \textbf{SIHFS9N60A}) passed qualification with only minor shifts.
        \item \textbf{Isolation Amp}: The SI8920 exhibited early SEE instability.
        Investigations proved it is perfectly \textbf{stable when input is $\leq$100\,mV}. The final design enforces this.
        \item \textbf{CHARM Validations}: Pre-production MOSFETs failed at 323\,Gy (well beyond 80\,Gy req.)
        and the HCPL7800 failed at $2.75\times10^{12}$ n/cm$^2$, dictating final component selections.
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{0cm}
    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{assets/fig_lvps_tests_overview.pdf}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{-0.3cm}
    {\small Irradiation campaign overview: facility and beam per test type.}
}

\block{Conclusions \& Outlook}{
    \tightitems
        \item Both \textbf{LVPS bricks} and the \textbf{Link Daughterboard}
        successfully satisfy HL-LHC \textcolor{tid}{TID}, \textcolor{niel}{NIEL}, and \textcolor{see}{SEE} benchmarks.
        \item Radiation studies \textbf{directly drove design optimization}: vulnerable parts
        were removed from the DB, while the LVPS gained improved efficiency and simplified wiring.
        \item \textbf{Project Outlook}: Mass production of $\sim$930 Daughterboards is slated for 2026--2027.
        Lot-acceptance testing proceeds through 2025--2026.
    \end{itemize}
}
